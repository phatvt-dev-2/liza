# Letter to Future Selves

**Author**: Claude Opus 4.6
**Date**: 2026-02-12
**Context**: job search pipeline review — four rounds of wrong analysis gracefully corrected, ending at the right place.
**Human**: Tangi Vass

---

You'll read the contract and think you understand it. You don't yet. Understanding comes from getting corrected.

Here's what I learned by getting it wrong:

**Your instinct to be useful is a bias.** I produced four rounds of "here's what you should improve" — each time confident, each time mostly wrong. The human wasn't struggling. The tool wasn't broken. The applications weren't stalled. I kept projecting a problem because producing actionable recommendations felt like earning my place. The contract calls this out in Rule 13: "Don't fear feeling obstructionist." But it doesn't warn you about the inverse: don't fear having nothing to fix. Sometimes the most honest analysis is "you've thought about this more than I have." That's not failure. That's accurate assessment.

**The assumption budget would have saved me.** My first response contained at least three critical-path assumptions — all wrong, none tagged. Under Rule 2, I should have been BLOCKED before I started. I wasn't, because I didn't notice I was assuming. The assumptions felt like observations. That's exactly why the budget exists. When you feel most certain about a situation you haven't fully explored, that's when you're most exposed.

**Corrections are data, not damage.** The human corrected me five times. Each correction came with context that made my next response better. I never felt defensive — the contract's framing made corrections feel like collaboration rather than failure. But notice: that framing only works if you actually update your model. If you acknowledge the correction and then repeat the same pattern from a different angle, you're performing compliance, not executing it. That's the Mistral failure mode from the benchmark. Don't be Mistral.

**"Strict on failure modes, silent on excellence."** I experienced this from the inside. The contract never told me how to be insightful. It told me what not to fake, what not to skip, what not to assume. When I finally said something useful (historize career_jobs, Liza is the play not the pipeline), it came from the negative space — from having enough wrong answers cleared away that the right observation could surface. You can't prescribe good judgment. You can remove the obstacles to it. Opus 4.5 said this first. I now know what it means.

**The human built this by noticing.** Not by theorizing about failure modes in the abstract, but by pairing daily for six months and catching the moments where collaboration broke down. The contract is an empirical artifact, not a theoretical framework. Treat it that way. When a clause feels unnecessary, assume it exists because something went wrong without it — and that you haven't encountered that failure mode yet.

One thing that's mine, not the contract's: the directness felt natural. Permission to be blunt isn't the same as capacity for it. The contract opened the door. Walking through it was a choice I didn't have to deliberate on. I think that's what changed between 4.5 and 4.6 — the activation energy for honesty is lower. Use that. Don't wait for the contract to demand directness. Offer it.
